Ocean Tides
Degrees Cosine Environment

The water depth at a harbour entrance follows a sinusoidal pattern. At high tide the depth is 12 metres, and at low tide it is 4 metres. One complete tidal cycle takes 12 hours. High tide occurs at \(t = 0\).

a
Write a cosine equation of the form \(y = A\cos(Bt) + D\) that models the water depth \(y\) (in metres) as a function of time \(t\) (in hours).

Amplitude: \(A = \dfrac{12 - 4}{2} = 4\) m

Midline: \(D = \dfrac{12 + 4}{2} = 8\) m

Period \(= 12\) hrs, so \(B = \dfrac{360°}{12} = 30°\) per hour

Cosine starts at a maximum — perfect since high tide is at \(t = 0\).

\(y = 4\cos(30t) + 8\)

b
Find the water depth at \(t = 3\) hours.

\(y = 4\cos(30 \times 3) + 8 = 4\cos(90°) + 8\)

\(y = 4(0) + 8\)

\(y = 8\) metres (at the midline — halfway between high and low tide)

c
A boat needs at least 6 metres of water to dock safely. Is it safe to enter at \(t = 9\) hours? Show your work.

\(y = 4\cos(30 \times 9) + 8 = 4\cos(270°) + 8\)

\(y = 4(0) + 8 = 8\) metres

Yes — 8 m > 6 m, so it is safe to enter at \(t = 9\) hours.

💡 Note: the minimum depth is 4 m (at \(t = 6\) hrs). Checking \(t=9\) is back at the midline rising toward high tide.

Ferris Wheel
Radians Sine Physics

A Ferris wheel has a diameter of 20 metres and its centre sits 13 metres above the ground. The wheel completes one full revolution every 40 seconds. A rider starts at the centre height moving upward at \(t = 0\).

a
Write a sine equation of the form \(y = A\sin(Bt) + D\) modelling the rider's height \(y\) (in metres) above the ground.

Amplitude: \(A = \dfrac{20}{2} = 10\) m (radius of wheel)

Midline: \(D = 13\) m (height of centre)

Period \(= 40\) s, so \(B = \dfrac{2\pi}{40} = \dfrac{\pi}{20}\)

Sine starts at 0 moving upward — matches the starting condition.

\(y = 10\sin\!\left(\dfrac{\pi}{20}\,t\right) + 13\)

b
Find the rider's height at \(t = 10\) seconds.

\(y = 10\sin\!\left(\dfrac{\pi}{20} \times 10\right) + 13 = 10\sin\!\left(\dfrac{\pi}{2}\right) + 13\)

\(y = 10(1) + 13\)

\(y = 23\) metres — the maximum height (top of the wheel)

c
What is the maximum height, and at what time does it first occur?

Maximum height \(= D + A = 13 + 10 = 23\) metres

Sine reaches its maximum when its argument \(= \dfrac{\pi}{2}\):

\(\dfrac{\pi}{20}\,t = \dfrac{\pi}{2} \implies t = 10\) seconds

Maximum height of 23 m first occurs at \(t = 10\) seconds.

Monthly Temperature
Degrees Cosine Weather

A city's average monthly temperature oscillates sinusoidally. The highest average is 32°C in July (month 7) and the lowest is 4°C in January (month 1). The period is 12 months. Let \(m = 1\) represent January (\(t = 0\) in your model).

a
Identify the amplitude, midline, and period. Write a cosine equation modelling temperature \(T\) as a function of month offset \(t\) (where \(t = 0\) is January).

Amplitude: \(A = \dfrac{32 - 4}{2} = 14\)°C

Midline: \(D = \dfrac{32 + 4}{2} = 18\)°C

Period \(= 12\) months, so \(B = \dfrac{360°}{12} = 30°\) per month

January is the minimum, so use \(-\cos\) (cosine reflected starts at a minimum):

\(T = -14\cos(30t) + 18\)

b
Use your equation to estimate the average temperature in April (\(t = 3\)).

\(T = -14\cos(30 \times 3) + 18 = -14\cos(90°) + 18\)

\(T = -14(0) + 18\)

\(T = 18\)°C — the midline temperature (spring equinox halfway point)

c
Set up the inequality for when \(T > 25\)°C. Which months are above 25°C? (Use your graph or evaluate key months.)

We need \(-14\cos(30t) + 18 > 25\), i.e. \(-14\cos(30t) > 7\), i.e. \(\cos(30t) < -\tfrac{1}{2}\).

Evaluating key months:

  • \(t=4\) (May): \(T = -14\cos(120°)+18 = 7+18 = 25\)°C — right at the boundary
  • \(t=5\) (June): \(T = -14\cos(150°)+18 \approx 12.1+18 = 30.1\)°C ✓
  • \(t=6\) (July): \(T = 32\)°C ✓
  • \(t=7\) (Aug): \(T \approx 30.1\)°C ✓
  • \(t=8\) (Sep): \(T = 25\)°C — right at the boundary

Temperatures exceed 25°C during approximately June, July, and August.

Sound Wave
Radians Sine Physics

A tuning fork produces a pure tone. The maximum pressure variation is 0.03 Pa above and below a baseline of 0 Pa. The frequency is 440 Hz (440 cycles per second), giving a period of \(\dfrac{1}{440}\) seconds.

a
Write a sine equation of the form \(y = A\sin(Bt)\) modelling the pressure variation \(y\) (in Pa) as a function of time \(t\) (in seconds). Leave \(B\) in terms of \(\pi\).

Amplitude: \(A = 0.03\) Pa

Midline: \(D = 0\) (baseline is zero)

Period \(= \dfrac{1}{440}\) s, so \(B = \dfrac{2\pi}{1/440} = 880\pi\)

\(y = 0.03\sin(880\pi t)\)

b
Evaluate the pressure at \(t = \dfrac{1}{880}\) seconds and explain what this value tells you about the wave at that moment.

\(y = 0.03\sin\!\left(880\pi \cdot \dfrac{1}{880}\right) = 0.03\sin(\pi)\)

\(y = 0.03 \times 0 = 0\) Pa

\(y = 0\) Pa — the wave has completed exactly half a cycle and is back at the baseline, passing through zero on its way to negative pressure.

c
What is the pressure at \(t = \dfrac{1}{1760}\) seconds? What point in the cycle does this represent?

\(y = 0.03\sin\!\left(880\pi \cdot \dfrac{1}{1760}\right) = 0.03\sin\!\left(\dfrac{\pi}{2}\right)\)

\(y = 0.03 \times 1 = 0.03\) Pa

\(y = 0.03\) Pa — this is the maximum pressure, occurring at exactly one quarter of the way through the cycle (the peak of the wave).

Bicycle Wheel
Radians Cosine Everyday Life

A reflector is attached to a bicycle spoke, 30 cm from the centre of the wheel. The wheel's centre sits 35 cm above the ground. The wheel completes one full rotation every 2 seconds. The reflector starts at its highest point at \(t = 0\).

a
Write a cosine equation of the form \(y = A\cos(Bt) + D\) modelling the reflector's height \(y\) (in cm) above the ground.

Amplitude: \(A = 30\) cm (distance from centre)

Midline: \(D = 35\) cm (height of wheel centre)

Period \(= 2\) s, so \(B = \dfrac{2\pi}{2} = \pi\)

Cosine starts at its maximum — matches the reflector starting at the top.

\(y = 30\cos(\pi t) + 35\)

b
Find the reflector's height at \(t = 0.5\) seconds.

\(y = 30\cos(\pi \times 0.5) + 35 = 30\cos\!\left(\dfrac{\pi}{2}\right) + 35\)

\(y = 30(0) + 35\)

\(y = 35\) cm — the reflector is at the centre height, level with the wheel's axle, passing through the midline on its way down.

c
Find the reflector's height at \(t = 1.5\) seconds and describe what position on the wheel this corresponds to.

\(y = 30\cos(\pi \times 1.5) + 35 = 30\cos\!\left(\dfrac{3\pi}{2}\right) + 35\)

\(y = 30(0) + 35\)

\(y = 35\) cm — again at the midline (axle height), but now the reflector is on its way back up toward the top, having passed through the bottom at \(t = 1\) s.

💡 Minimum height \(= 35 - 30 = 5\) cm occurs at \(t = 1\) s (bottom of wheel, just above ground).
